# Lesson 1: The Basics of Credit Cards

*An introduction to how credit cards actually work, the key terms you need to know, and why understanding the system makes you a smarter user.*

---

## How a Credit Card Transaction Actually Works

Most people think: swipe → money moves → done. But there's a whole pipeline behind that swipe, and understanding it changes how you think about your card.

### The Transaction Flow

1. **Authorization** — You swipe or tap. The merchant's terminal sends a request to their acquirer (the bank that processes their payments). The acquirer routes it to your card network (Visa, Mastercard, RuPay, Amex). The network routes it to your issuer (HDFC, SBI, Chase, whoever issued your card). Your issuer checks: Do you have enough credit? Is the card valid? Is this suspicious? If yes, they send back an authorization code. Money hasn't moved yet — just a promise.

2. **Settlement** — At the end of the day (or batch cycle), the merchant submits all authorized transactions. The acquirer collects them and sends them through the network. Now money moves: issuer → network → acquirer → merchant. This is when your available credit actually decreases.

3. **Statement Generation** — Your issuer tallies all settled transactions for the billing cycle and generates your statement. This shows your statement balance — what you owe if you want to avoid interest.

4. **Payment** — You pay by the due date. If you pay the full statement balance, you pay zero interest. If you pay less, interest kicks in on the unpaid amount.

**Why this matters:** The gap between authorization and settlement (usually 1-3 days) is why your "pending transactions" don't immediately show in your statement. The gap between statement generation and due date (the grace period, usually 20-25 days) is your interest-free window.

## Key Terms, Actually Explained

### APR (Annual Percentage Rate)

This is the interest rate on your card, expressed as a yearly percentage. But you're not charged interest annually — it's applied daily or monthly to your balance.

**The math:**
- APR: 24%
- Daily Periodic Rate: 24% ÷ 365 = 0.0658% per day
- If you carry a ₹10,000 balance for 30 days: ₹10,000 × 0.000658 × 30 = ₹197 in interest

**The grace period trick:** If you pay your full statement balance by the due date, you pay zero interest. The APR only applies to carried balances. This is why paying in full every month is the golden rule.

### Credit Limit vs. Available Credit

- **Credit Limit:** The maximum you can borrow. Set by the issuer based on your income, credit history, and their risk appetite.
- **Available Credit:** Credit limit minus current balance minus pending authorizations.

**Utilization ratio:** Your balance ÷ credit limit. If your limit is ₹2,00,000 and you spend ₹40,000, your utilization is 20%. Below 30% is considered healthy for your credit score. Maxing out your card (high utilization) signals risk to credit bureaus, even if you pay in full.

### Statement Balance vs. Current Balance

- **Statement Balance:** What you owed at the moment the statement was generated. This is the number to pay to avoid interest.
- **Current Balance:** What you owe right now, including new purchases after the statement cut.

**Example:**
- Statement generated March 1: ₹50,000
- You buy groceries March 5: ₹3,000
- Current balance on March 6: ₹53,000
- To avoid interest, pay ₹50,000 (statement balance), not ₹53,000. The ₹3,000 will appear on your next statement.

### Minimum Payment

This is the smallest amount you can pay to avoid late fees and keep your account in good standing. It's usually 5% of your statement balance or a fixed minimum (₹200-500), whichever is higher.

**The trap:** If you only pay the minimum, the remaining balance accrues interest. And interest compounds. A ₹50,000 balance with 24% APR and 5% minimum payment takes **over 4 years** to pay off, costing you nearly ₹20,000 in interest.

## How Card Issuers Make Money

Understanding the business model makes you a smarter consumer. Card issuers have three primary revenue streams:

### 1. Interchange Fees

Every time you use your card, the merchant pays 1.5-3% of the transaction value to the card ecosystem. This is split between the network (Visa, Mastercard), the acquirer, and the issuer.

**Implication:** Merchants factor this cost into prices. Some pass it to consumers via card surcharges. In India, you'll see "2% extra on card payments" at some shops. In the US, this is built into shelf prices, so cash payers effectively subsidize card users.

### 2. Interest

This is the big one. Issuers earn interest on carried balances. If you pay in full every month, you're a "transactor" — they make money off you via interchange. If you carry a balance, you're a "revolver" — they make money off you via interest.

**Industry secret:** Revolvers are more profitable than transactors. This is why issuers offer generous rewards — they're betting some portion of users will carry balances and pay interest.

### 3. Annual Fees and Other Charges

Some cards charge annual fees (₹500 to ₹10,000+ in India; $95 to $695+ in the US for premium cards). Other revenue: late fees, over-limit fees, foreign transaction fees, cash advance fees.

**The rewards game:** Premium cards with high annual fees often offer perks (lounge access, travel credits, accelerated points) that can exceed the fee — if you use them. The issuer's bet: most people won't optimize fully.

## Credit Card vs. Debit Card

They look the same. They tap the same. But they're fundamentally different.

| | Credit Card | Debit Card |
|---|---|---|
| **Source of funds** | Issuer's money (loan) | Your bank account |
| **Fraud liability** | Limited (₹0 in most cases if reported promptly) | Varies — your money is already gone |
| **Credit score impact** | Builds credit history | No impact |
| **Float** | 20-50 days interest-free | None — instant debit |
| **Rewards** | Often 1-5% back | Rarely offered |
| **Overspending risk** | Higher — borrowed money | Lower — limited to your balance |

**In India:** Credit cards are still a status symbol. In the US, they're a credit-building necessity — you can't get a mortgage without a credit history, and you can't build credit history without credit products. This creates a chicken-and-egg problem for newcomers.

## Building Credit History: Why It Matters

In the US, your credit score (FICO score, ranging from 300 to 850) determines:
- Whether you get approved for loans and credit cards
- What interest rates you're offered
- Whether landlords rent to you
- Sometimes, whether employers hire you (for certain roles)

**How credit cards build credit:**
1. **Payment history (35% of FICO):** On-time payments every month build trust. One missed payment can drop your score by 100+ points.
2. **Credit utilization (30%):** Keeping balances low relative to limits shows discipline.
3. **Length of history (15%):** Older accounts help. Don't close your oldest card unless you have to.
4. **Credit mix (10%):** Having both revolving credit (cards) and installment loans (mortgage, auto) helps.
5. **New credit (10%):** Too many applications in a short period signals risk.

**In India:** Credit bureaus (CIBIL, Equifax, Experian, CRIF High Mark) operate similarly but scores are less ingrained in daily life. This is changing — more landlords, employers, and insurers are starting to check credit scores.

## A Real Example: The Minimum Payment Trap

Let's say you spend ₹50,000 on your credit card. Your APR is 24% (2% monthly). Your minimum payment is 5% of the statement balance.

| Month | Balance | Minimum Payment (5%) | Interest (2%) | Balance After Payment |
|-------|---------|---------------------|---------------|----------------------|
| 1 | ₹50,000 | ₹2,500 | ₹950 | ₹48,450 |
| 6 | ₹42,460 | ₹2,123 | ₹806 | ₹41,143 |
| 12 | ₹35,790 | ₹1,790 | ₹680 | ₹34,680 |
| 24 | ₹24,130 | ₹1,207 | ₹458 | ₹23,381 |
| 36 | ₹14,520 | ₹726 | ₹276 | ₹14,070 |
| 48 | ₹5,870 | ₹294 | ₹111 | ₹5,687 |
| 53 | ₹0 | — | — | Paid off |

**Total time to pay off: 53 months (4.4 years)**
**Total interest paid: ₹19,700**
**Original purchase: ₹50,000**
**You actually paid: ₹69,700**

All because you paid the minimum instead of the full balance.

**The lesson:** Pay your statement balance in full, every month. No exceptions. The minimum payment is a trap, not a feature.

## Summary: What You Should Remember

1. **A credit card is a short-term loan** with a grace period. Use the grace period, and it's free credit. Carry a balance, and it's expensive debt.

2. **Know your terms:** APR, credit limit, statement balance, minimum payment. These aren't fine print — they're the rules of the game.

3. **Issuers profit from interchange and interest.** You profit by paying in full and collecting rewards.

4. **Credit vs. debit:** Credit builds your financial identity. Debit is just a convenient way to spend your own money.

5. **The minimum payment is a trap.** Always pay the full statement balance.
